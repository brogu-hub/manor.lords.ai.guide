# Building a Historically Accurate Medieval Village & Castle Design

Source: Steam Workshop Guide by rogue_26 (MA in Medieval Archaeology)

## Colonizing the Land

Heavy plows enabled settlement of previously unfarmable land. A 12th-century population surge pushed people into marginal territories. Medieval lords and the Church saw this as opportunity — the Church viewed it as Christianizing "sinful" lands. New towns were chartered with tax incentives for peasants. Many European towns bear names like "Newton" or "Neuville" reflecting this expansion. The process involved clearing land, felling trees, constructing buildings, establishing commerce, and religious blessing by priests.

## Settlement Patterns & Hierarchy

Settlements follow feudal hierarchy: kings delegated to barons, who delegated to gentry, who delegated to peasants. Cities (towns with Cathedrals/Bishops) topped the hierarchy, supported by large villages, which supported specialized smaller communities — mines, quarries, forester camps, charcoal-burning operations.

### Town vs Village

Towns/cities support intellectual labor and an emerging middle class (law, guilds, banking, education). Villages primarily support agricultural and extractive labor.

### Settlement Strategy (Central Place Theory)

- Create farming hamlets exporting grain to main settlements
- Establish worker camps near rich resources
- Develop barter networks connecting to broader commerce
- Focus tech-tree points on branches matching abundant resources
- Spread farmhouses between two hamlets (4-5 houses each) to prevent excessive density

## Vernacular Architecture & Land Use

### Church Placement

Churches oriented toward the East, positioned near marketplaces but rarely center-stage. Typically established at town entrances along roads, demarcating civilization boundaries and ensuring remote peasant access.

### Residential Design

Manor Lords excellently represents medieval lots and longhouses: stone footings, large timber frames with mortise-and-tenon joinery, wattle-and-daub infill. Long, narrow lots maximized economic use. The game avoids modern zoning-law separation between industry and housing, reflecting authentic medieval practice.

### Industry Placement (Critical for Layout)

Medieval practice divided industry into "smelly" and "non-smelly" categories:
- **Smelly industries** (tanners, potters, blacksmiths): Place OUTSIDE town on the eastern/downwind side
- **Non-smelly industries**: Can operate on residential properties
- Miasma theory meant bad odors were believed to cause disease

### Wind & Urban Layout

European winds prevail west-to-east:
- **West side**: Elite homes, administration, lord's castle — upwind, cleaner air
- **East side**: Industry, workshops, lower-class housing — downwind
- This pattern persists in modern communities

### Castle & Fortification Placement Guidelines

Based on historical medieval principles:
- Castle/manor house should be positioned on elevated ground when possible
- Place on the western (upwind) side of the settlement for cleaner air and strategic overview
- Castle should overlook the marketplace and main road for control
- Defensive walls should follow natural terrain features (rivers, hills, ridges)
- Keep granary and storehouse within or adjacent to fortified area for siege protection
- Stone construction requires significant resource stockpiling: plan quarry operations early
- Castle construction is a sign of surplus and stability — only attempt when basic needs are well-covered

### Market Infrastructure

- Open-air markets are functional but limited
- Covered markets increase storage capacity
- Position market at the crossroads of main routes for maximum trade flow
